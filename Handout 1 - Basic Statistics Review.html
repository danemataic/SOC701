Basic Vocabulary

Population – the set of all individuals of interest for a particular study

Parameter – a value, usually numeric, that describes a population. A parameter may be obtained from a single measurement, or it may be derived from a set of measurements from the population.

Sample – a subset of the population

Statistic – a value, usually numerical, that describes the sample. A sample may be obtained from a single measurement, or it may be derived from a set of measurements from the sample.

Descriptive Statistics – statistical procedures that are used to summarize, organize, and simplify data.

Inferential Statistics – statistics that consist of techniques that allow us to study samples and then make generalizations about the populations from which they were selected.

Sampling Error – the discrepancy, or amount of error, that exists between a sample statistics and the corresponding population parameter.

Variable – a characteristic or condition that changes or has different values for different individuals.

Constant – a characteristics or condition that does not vary but is the same for every individual.

Independent Variable – the variable that is manipulated by the researcher. In behavioral research the independent variable usually consists of the two (or more) treatment conditions to which subjects are exposed.

Dependent Variable – one that is observed for changes in order to assess the effect of the treatment.

  • Experiment to test the effect of alcohol on reaction time
  • See how many school absences last year, give flu shots, see how many absences this year.
  • Yearly income and education

Levels of Measurement

1. Nominal Scale – a set of categories that have different names. There are no quantitative distinctions between observations.

  • named category
  • no order
  • = categorical
  • ex: gender, political affiliation, religion

2. Ordinal Scale – consist of a set of categories that are organized in an ordered sequence. Measurements on an ordinal scale rank observations in terms of size or magnitude.

  • named categories which are ordered or ranked
  • the distinction between them is not the same
  • Likert scale
  • ex: president, vice-president, favorite show, color, etc.

3. Interval Scale – an ordered set of categories with the additional requirements that the categories form a series of intervals that are exactly the same size.

  • named categories which are ordered or ranked with equal intervals
  • arbitrary zero
  • ex: scores on a test, temperature

4. Ratio Scale – same as interval, but with an absolute zero

  • named categories which are ordered or ranked with equal intervals
  • non-arbitrary zero
  • ex: height, length, age, income
Note

Interval and ratio behave the same statistically, so you will often find the two linked together as interval/ratio.*

Discrete and Continuous Variables

Discrete Variables – a discrete variable consists of separate, indivisible categories. No values can exist between two neighboring categories.

  • number of children

Continuous Variables – a continuous variable is divisible into an infinite number of fractional parts.

  • time, age, length

Levels of Analysis

Micro-level – the smallest unit, the local or individual level.

Macro-level – the largest unit, generally considers the social structure.

Meso-level – considered a mid-range unit, between Micro and Macro (e.g. organizations or social networks).

Describing Data

Simple Distribution – scores (Xs) are arranged in some order. This works fine, but is hard for large samples.

Simple Frequency Distribution (f) – how many unique Xs. No loss of information.

Group Frequency Distribution – loss of information, know the interval but not the individual scores.

Graphs – there are many different ways to graphically represent data (here are two of the most common in statistics).

  1. Bar Graph – when you are presenting the frequency distribution for data from a nominal or an ordinal scale, the graph is constructed so that there is some space between the bars.

  2. Histograms – when a frequency distribution graph is showing data from an interval or a ratio scale, the bars are drawn so that adjacent bars touch each other.

Central Tendency

A statistical measure to determine a single score that defines the center of a distribution. The goal of central tendency is to find the single score that is most typical or most representative of the entire group.

The Mode – the score or category that has the greatest frequency

Helpful when using nominal scale variables, discrete data (if you calculate the mean, you get the funny 2.4 children per family type of data, whereas if you use the mode, you can say most families had 2 children.)

The Median – the score that divides a distribution exactly in half. Exactly 50% of the individuals in a distribution have scores at or below the median. Calculation is the same for population and sample. * When N is an odd number, list the scores in order the median score is the middle term. * When N is an even number, list the scores in order and average the two middle scores. * In general, (n+1)/2 \rightarrow the ^{th} value

Calculate the Median Value

X: 4, 8, 5, 7, 5, 6, 1, 5, 7

What is the Median value of the following: 4, 8, 5, 7, 5, 6, 1, 5, 7

The Median value is 5

When we have an even number, we add up the middle terms and divide

Such as for the following: 8, 7, 7, 6, 5, 5, 4, 1

We add 6 and 5 (the two middle terms) and divide by two

\frac{(6+5)}{2} = \frac{11}{2} = 5.5

Helpful for talking about things where the data is skewed or there are extreme values - housing prices, for example. We don’t calculate a median for nominal data.

The Mean – the sum of the scores divided by the number of scores (can be thought of as a balance point)

X: 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 \sum{X} = 15, N = 5 \mu = \frac{\sum{X}}{N} = \frac{15}{5} = 3

x: 5, 4, 3, 2, 1 \sum{X} = 15, N = 5 \bar{x} = \frac{\sum{x}}{n} = \frac{15}{5} = 3

Variability

Variability provides a quantitative measure of the degree to which scores in a distribution are spread out or clustered together. In order words, how scores differ from one another.

Range – most general measure of variability. Computed by subtracting the lowest score from the highest score range = highest - lowest

There is also the IQR, or Inter-Quartile Range. Difference between the first and third quartiles

Standard Deviation – the average amount of variability; the average distance from the mean.

SD = \sqrt{\frac{\sum{(X_i - \bar{X})^2}}{n-1}}

Variance – the average squared distance from the mean.
